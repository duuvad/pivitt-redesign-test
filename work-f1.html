F1 Drive

A charity karting Grand Prix for the music industry at F1 Drive London, bringing together Tottenham Hotspur and Live Nation in support of Nordoff and Robbins. We created the naming system and the visual identity, from the event name to the campaign that ran across the city.

The campaign live at scale.

The brief

Name and dress a charity Grand Prix.

F1 Drive London was hosting its first charity karting Grand Prix, a music-industry event in support of Nordoff and Robbins, the UK’s largest music therapy charity, in partnership with Tottenham Hotspur and Live Nation. It needed a name that could carry the occasion and grow into a series, and a visual identity strong enough to fill a billboard and sell out the grid.

The name

An F1 naming system, built to scale.

We started from Formula 1’s own naming logic, a place and a Grand Prix, and built it into a three-part system: a constant, a partner-led event name, and a year. It gave the inaugural race a name of its own and the organisers a structure that could return and grow, season after season. The inaugural event ran as the F1 Drive Music Therapy Grand Prix.

The look

One lead creative, built to travel.

The lead creative was broken into a grid of reusable elements so it could carry across every touchpoint, from a full poster to a single banner, without losing its character.

Evolution of the creative

From retro homage to the grid.

The first direction was a nostalgic one, a vintage Grand Prix poster brought back for a modern karting event. From there the creative grew bolder and more photographic, built around the driver and the lights of the grid, in two systems we called Blend and Grid.

Where it started: the retro concept.
Evolution into the Blend and Grid systems.

The campaign

A Grand Prix invite, turned up.

The final campaign led with a bold, photographic invite, the driver, the helmet and the neon of the grid, carried into a flexible set of colourways and a full event deck.

Four colourways for the invite.
The full event deck.

In the world

Across the city, on the day.

The identity rolled out across outdoor sites in the run-up to race day, from digital billboards to bus-stop posters.

Bus-stop poster, JCDecaux.

The outcome

A one-off with a series in its DNA.

F1 Drive went into its first charity Grand Prix with a name that works like a Formula 1 title and a campaign that earned its place on a billboard. The system was built so that the next race, and the one after, already have somewhere to go.
